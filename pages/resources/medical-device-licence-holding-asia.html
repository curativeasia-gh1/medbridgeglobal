Medical Device Licence Holding — Distributor-Independent Registration in Asia

A practical guide for manufacturers on how to protect their regulatory assets in Asia through licence holding and distributor-independent registration structures across Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia and Thailand.

The Problem with Distributor-Held Registrations

In most Asian markets, medical devices must be registered with the national health authority before they can be marketed. The entity that holds the product registration — the registrant, licence holder or authorised representative — carries legal responsibility for that product in the market.

Many manufacturers entering Asia allow their commercial distributor to hold the product registration. This is often the path of least resistance: the distributor is already incorporated locally, they have the relationships and they are motivated to get the product registered quickly.

The problem arises when the commercial relationship changes. If the distributor holds the registration and the relationship ends, the manufacturer faces a difficult choice: negotiate a transfer with the outgoing distributor (who may not cooperate), wait for a new registration under the new distributor (which can take months or years), or leave the market entirely. In the meantime, the product cannot legally be sold.

What is Licence Holding?

Licence holding refers to an arrangement where a neutral, independent entity holds the product registration or marketing authorisation on behalf of the manufacturer — separately from the commercial distributor. The licence holder fulfils the regulatory requirements of the local market (local representative, authorised importer, responsible person) without being involved in the commercial sale of the product.

This structure allows manufacturers to:

  • Retain control over their regulatory assets
  • Change commercial distributors without affecting their registration
  • Appoint multiple distributors if the market and regulatory structure permits
  • Exit a distributor relationship without market access disruption
  • Maintain regulatory continuity during company acquisitions or restructuring

Licence Holding Across the Four Markets

🇭🇰 Hong Kong

Hong Kong does not operate a product registration system for most medical devices in the same way as Singapore or Malaysia. However, for certain regulated products, a Local Responsible Person must be appointed. Medbridge can act as Local Responsible Person and manage product notifications, import coordination and regulatory correspondence independently of the commercial distributor.

🇸🇬 Singapore

Singapore's HSA framework allows a Product Registrant to hold the product registration independently of the commercial distributor. The Product Registrant must hold a valid Dealer's Licence and be a Singapore-registered entity. This structure is well-established in Singapore and used widely by manufacturers who want to separate regulatory control from commercial distribution.

🇲🇾 Malaysia

In Malaysia, the Local Authorised Representative (LAR) holds the establishment licence and can hold or co-manage the product registration. The LAR can be an entity completely separate from the commercial distributor, and this separation is increasingly used by manufacturers who want to protect their registration continuity. The LAR must be incorporated in Malaysia and hold an MDA Establishment Licence for the relevant activities.

🇹🇭 Thailand

Thailand's Thai FDA framework is more restrictive — the licence holder is typically more closely tied to the importer. However, independent structures are possible in certain configurations and are being reviewed under ongoing regulatory harmonisation. Medbridge reviews each product and market combination individually to advise on the most appropriate structure available under current Thai FDA rules.

Registration Transfer Planning

If a manufacturer currently has registrations held by a distributor and wants to move to an independent structure, registration transfer planning is essential. This typically involves:

  • Reviewing the current registration documentation and ownership position
  • Assessing the transfer mechanism available in each market
  • Timing the transfer to avoid gaps in market access
  • Coordinating with the outgoing holder and health authority
  • Preparing the incoming licence holder entity and documentation
  • Maintaining import and distribution continuity during the transition

Transfer timelines vary significantly by market. Some transfers can be completed in weeks; others may require new applications that take months. Early planning is critical.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is medical device licence holding?

Medical device licence holding refers to an arrangement where a neutral, independent entity holds the product registration or marketing authorisation on behalf of the manufacturer — separately from the commercial distributor. This protects the manufacturer's regulatory investment and ensures continuity if the commercial distribution arrangement changes.

Why would a manufacturer use a licence holder instead of giving the registration to the distributor?

When a distributor holds the product registration, the manufacturer's products are effectively tied to that distributor. If the commercial relationship ends, the registration may need to be transferred — which can take months or years. An independent licence holder avoids this dependency, giving the manufacturer freedom to change distributors without disrupting market access.

What happens to my registration if I change distributors?

If the distributor holds your registration, changing distributors requires a registration transfer that can take months and may interrupt your ability to import or sell. If an independent licence holder holds the registration, changing the commercial distributor does not affect the registration — the product can continue to be imported and sold without interruption.

Is distributor-independent licence holding permitted in Singapore and Malaysia?

Yes. Both Singapore and Malaysia have well-established frameworks that allow an independent entity to hold the product registration separately from the commercial distributor. This structure is used widely and is a normal part of professional regulatory practice in both markets.
